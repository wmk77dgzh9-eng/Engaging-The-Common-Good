Overview

Where Faith Meets the Climate Crisis

The environment is one of the biggest cultural issues of our time, and the Catholic Church has stepped right into the middle of it. This project looks at Development and Peace, Caritas Canada, the official international solidarity organization of the Catholic Church in Canada, and how its work for ecological justice supports the common good and lives out the principles of Catholic Social Teaching (CST).

The Engagement

What Development and Peace Actually Does

Founded in 1967 by the Canadian Conference of Catholic Bishops and now the Canadian member of Caritas Internationalis, Development and Peace works in partnership with grassroots groups in 34 countries. On the environment, its engagement takes three main forms:

🌾

Climate Resilient Farming

It funds and supports local partners in the Global South to develop farming that can survive drought, floods and a changing climate, protecting food and livelihoods for the communities least responsible for the crisis.

🌳

Defending Fragile Bioregions

Through campaigns like For Our Common Home, it stood with Indigenous peoples of the Amazon to protect one of the planet's most vital ecosystems, linking the "cry of the earth" to the "cry of the poor."

⚖️

Holding Companies Accountable

Since 2008 its members have pressed the Canadian government to hold Canadian mining corporations accountable for environmental and human rights abuses abroad, a distinctly Canadian responsibility.

"A true ecological approach always becomes a social approach; it must integrate questions of justice in debates on the environment, so as to hear both the cry of the earth and the cry of the poor."

Pope Francis, Laudato Si' (2015), §49, the encyclical that inspires this work

Why does this matter so much in Canada? Because a majority of the world's mining corporations are headquartered or raise capital here. Communities harmed by Canadian mining abroad find it almost impossible to seek justice in Canadian courts, so Development and Peace gives Canadian Catholics a way to respond.

Campaigns for Creation

Supporting the Common Good

How This Serves Everyone, Especially in Canada

The common good means the conditions that let every person and community flourish. By linking Canadian choices to their effects on the world's poorest, Development and Peace builds up the common good far beyond Church walls:

The world's most vulnerable

Global South and Indigenous communities, who did the least to cause climate change but suffer its worst effects, gain support, a voice, and defenders of their land and rights.

Canadian youth & students

School programs, THINKfast fundraisers and parish campaigns form young Canadians as responsible, globally aware citizens (exactly what this course calls a "Responsible Citizen").

Canadian society & democracy

By pushing for laws that hold corporations accountable, it strengthens justice and ethics in Canada's own economy and institutions.

Future generations

Caring for our "common home" today protects the planet handed on to everyone who comes after us, the heart of ecological responsibility.

Catholic Social Teaching

The Principles Upheld

Development and Peace names ten principles of Catholic Social Teaching at the core of its work. Its environmental engagement upholds these ones most clearly. Click each to see how.

The Earth is sacred and all of creation has intrinsic value. By defending bioregions and promoting sustainable farming, Development and Peace treats protecting "our common home for future generations" as a direct response to God, the exact principle at the centre of Laudato Si'.

Because climate change hits hardest the people "least responsible for it," the organization insists we "must reject solutions that leave out the poor." Its partners work first with the most vulnerable communities, putting the poor at the centre, not the margins.

As a "democratic movement for international solidarity," it links Canadians directly to partners in 34 countries, acting on the belief that we are one human family responsible for one another across borders.

Its advocacy for corporate accountability and climate action aims at conditions that let all people and future generations flourish, not private profit. The bishops call this "the sustenance and flourishing of life for all beings."

Every person is made in God's image, so defending communities' land, water and rights against environmental harm is really defending their God given dignity, the foundation of all Catholic Social Teaching.

Rather than imposing solutions, it backs "people led, ecologically sustainable alternatives," empowering local communities to take charge of their own development and respecting their right to participate in decisions that affect them.
